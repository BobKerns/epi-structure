@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone You have to take population structure into account. If
populations were homogenous, your case would be solid. Superspreader events are
evidence of non-homogenous structure. When superspreader events connect to more
superspreader events, things change.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone It is very hard to model population structure. There is a
fundamental data problem. The best data is from cell phone data, as was done at the time of
the Sturgis motorcycle rally, for example.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone The Minnesota team used compartments by age, but it was
limited in inter-compartment contacts; for example, we know schools facilitate spread
(though that was denied by some). Grandparents living with school-age children or children
visiting in nursing homes become important.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone Simple extrapolation is, of course baseline, but we should
keep in mind the limitations of extrapolating into new contexts.
Epidemiology isn't as simple as it seems. The Kermack-McKendrick SIR model assumes
homogeneity, and works well enough in a homogenous subgroup.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone (Whiteboard I found one day when my daughter was in 11th
grade, back in 2016. She's a disease ecologist now).
The long incubation time means we'll need some time to be sure that extrapolating from
Argentina is a good approximation. We can hope.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone But the early phase of an epidemic is approximately
exponential. Extrapolating exponentials is difficult; even a small difference in the exponent
compounds quickly. Here, the exponent is Rₜ, the value of R at a point in time in a specific
context.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone Rₜ does not depend on just the virus. It depends on behavior—
how many contacts an infected person has—and environment. A cruise ship is not a great
environment but at least has ventilation; a dorm with poor ventilation is worse (and suggests
many contacts as well).
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone Superspreader events are contexts with a high Rₜ. But again,
this is a local, homogenous context. Everyone in the group is connected.
But now consider multiple of these groups. Let's keep it "simple"
, and assume the group is
exposed once, then they separate.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone We can think of these groups and their infected members as a
single entity for our present purposes.
The key question becomes, what is the R₀ (baseline) and Rₜ (time-varying) value for
transmission between groups.
How do these groups connect?
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone Connections can be direct or indirect. A person can be a
member of multiple potential superspreader groups, or may be part of a longer chain of
transmission through the community.
For Argentina, this superspreader group R₀ and Rₜ were 0 (as far as I know).
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone That is, it did not reach a second superspreader group within
Argentina.
But the cruise ship is a second superspreader event. I would be very surprised if they are
connected; too much time has elapsed. More likely a separate zoonosis event.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone But the question now, taking this model into account
becomes, what is the structure of potential superspreader groups in Europe? In South
Africa?
This is the potential vulnerability. We do not want to learn that this R₀ is >1. We want to block
it before it becomes an issue.
@sportsnow41 @DrNeilStone And it is why we should not be overly-assured by the
Argentine experience. Somewhat reassured, certainly. It could be a lot worse!
But it does not warrant dismissal about what happens in a different population.
